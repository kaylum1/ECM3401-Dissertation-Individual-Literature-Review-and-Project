Do Not Track (DNT) Support Scan - Understanding Online Privacy

This page will break down and explains Do Not Track (DNT) support scans, what it means when a scan does not detect anything, how users can be exposed, and the scientific process behind how these scans work.

What is Do Not Track (DNT)?

Do Not Track (DNT) is a browser feature that lets people signal to websites that they prefer not to be tracked. When switched on, the browser adds the DNT: 1 HTTP header to each request, asking sites not to collect or share user information for advertising or analytics.

Although DNT was proposed as a web standard, it is not legally binding, and many sites overlook—or deliberately ignore the header.

While DNT was initially designed as a web standard, it is not legally enforceable, and many websites ignore it.

What Happens When a DNT Support Scan Shows Nothing?

If a scan does not detect Do Not Track support, it may indicate:

  • The website does not recognise or respect the DNT header.
  • The website collects and shares user data regardless of user preferences.
  • The website relies on third-party trackers that do not adhere to DNT.

A lack of DNT support means the website may track user behaviour even if users have opted out through their browser settings.

How Users are Left Exposed Without DNT Support

When websites ignore Do Not Track requests, users may face several risks:

  • Behavioral Tracking: Websites can track your browsing activity across different sites.
  • Targeted Advertising: Your data may be shared with advertisers, leading to invasive personalized ads.
  • Privacy Violations: Websites may store and analyze personal data without user consent.
  • Data Breaches: If collected data is not securely stored, it may be exposed in breaches.
  • Profiling: Companies can build detailed digital profiles of users, including their habits, preferences, and demographics.

How to Protect Yourself from Online Tracking

Because many sites ignore DNT, users should adopt extra safeguards:

  • Use Privacy-Focused Browsers: Firefox, Brave, or Tor Browser add stronger tracking defences.
  • Install Tracker-Blocking Extensions: Add-ons such as Privacy Badger or uBlock Origin block tracking scripts.
  • Disable Third-Party Cookies: Browser settings can block cookies from external domains.
  • Use a VPN: A virtual private network masks your IP address from trackers.
  • Regularly Clear Cookies: Deleting stored cookies removes long-term tracking data.
  • Opt Out of Data Collection: Some sites let you refine tracking choices in privacy settings.

The Science Behind Do Not Track Scanning

Do Not Track scanners check for DNT support by analyzing HTTP headers and website responses. The process includes:

  • Sending a DNT Request: The scanner makes an HTTP request that includes the DNT: 1 header.
  • Monitoring Server Response: It checks whether the site acknowledges or ignores the header.
  • Analysing Privacy Policies: Some tools examine the site’s privacy notice for DNT language.
  • Checking Tracking Behaviour: The scanner looks for tracking scripts that remain active.

Why Websites Should Support Do Not Track

Although DNT is voluntary, websites that respect it benefit in several ways:

  • Building User Trust: Transparency about tracking improves credibility.
  • Compliance with Privacy Laws: Supporting DNT aligns with GDPR, CCPA, and other data protection regulations.
  • Reducing Security Risks: Collecting less data minimizes exposure in case of a breach.
  • Improving User Experience: Some users prefer websites that do not track them for advertising purposes.
