Referrer & DNT Analysis Scan - Understanding Online Privacy

This page breaks down and explains how Referrer & DNT (Do Not Track) Analysis scans work, what it means when a scan does not detect any issues, how users can be exposed, and the scientific process behind these scans.

What is Referrer & DNT Analysis?

A Referrer & DNT analysis checks whether a site correctly handles two key browser signals:
The first is the referrer header: Reveals the URL of the page a visitor arrived from.
The second is DNT header (DNT: 1): Indicates the user does not wish to be tracked.

The scan verifies that a site neither leaks unnecessary referrer data nor ignores the user’s DNT preference.

What Happens When a Referrer & DNT Analysis Scan Shows Nothing?

If a scan does not detect any issues with Referrer headers or DNT signals, it may indicate some of the following:

  • The site strips or limits referrer information so it cannot identify the previous page.
  • DNT requests are honoured; tracking cookies or scripts are suppressed for opted-out users.
  • More subtle or disguised tracking tricks remained undetected by the scanner.

Even with a “no problems found” result, hidden techniques may still slip through, so periodic re-testing is advised.

How Users are Left Exposed Without Referrer and DNT Protection

If a site mishandles these signals, people may face:

  • Unintended Data Exposure: Referrer strings might reveal personal or sensitive details during page-to-page navigation.
  • Tracking Without Consent: Sites can ignore DNT and continue profiling visitors for ads or analytics.
  • Cross-Site Tracking: Third parties can follow users across multiple domains via referrer leaks.
  • Privacy Violations: Disregarding DNT can breach user expectations or even regulatory rules.

How to Protect Yourself from Referrer Leaks and Tracking

Users and website administrators can take the following steps to protect privacy and block unnecessary tracking:

  • Using Privacy-Centric Browsers: Firefox, Brave, or similar browsers send stronger DNT signals and limit referrers.
  • Installing Anti-Tracking Add-ons: Tools such as Privacy Badger or uBlock Origin block trackers and trim referrer headers.
  • Disabling or Shortening Referrers: Modern browsers offer settings or flags to scrub or anonymise referrer data.
  • Enabling DNT: Turn on the DNT option so compliant sites know not to track you.
  • Forcing HTTPS: Encrypted requests lessen the chance that referrer information is exposed in transit.

The Science Behind Referrer & DNT Scanning

Referrer & DNT scanners typically:

  • Monitor HTTP Requests: Capture outgoing traffic to inspect referrer fields for accidental data disclosure.
  • Check DNT Compliance: Users can observe whether the site suppresses tracking when the DNT: 1 header is present.
  • Test for Referrer Leaks: Try and navigate through various pages and domains to see if sensitive URLs leak via referrers.
  • Compliance with Privacy Policies: The scan also checks if the website's privacy policy aligns with the observed referrer and tracking behaviour.

Why Websites Should Respect Referrer and DNT Signals

Honouring these headers benefits both users and site operators:

  • Enhances Trust: Visitors feel safer on sites that uphold their privacy choices.
  • Meets Legal Obligations: Frameworks like GDPR and CCPA expect transparent handling of tracking preferences.
  • Reduces Data Exposure: Limiting referrer data cuts the risk of unintentional information leaks.
  • Promotes Ethical Practices: Following user signals fosters a more responsible and privacy-friendly web.
